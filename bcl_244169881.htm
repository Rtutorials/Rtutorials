The dataset is of 5,043 movies along with their actors, directors, genres, duration, gross, budget, facebook likes, IMDB rating, and other information. The data was retrieved from kaggle.com at https://www.kaggle.com/deepmatrix/imdb-­‐5000-­‐ movie-­‐dataset, scraped from IMDB.com by user Chuansun76. These movies include all the most popular films you would expect, and are predominantly American (75.4%). The vast majority of the films are recent (mean = 2002.47), as shown by the histogram below, and 4.1% are in Black and White.

The average movie length is 108 minutes.

The data needed some mild cleaning in order to remove duplicates and TV shows. problem that was apparent was the fact that some foreign movie budgets were unrealistically high. The issue was that the budget had not been converted to US Dollars, that’s why a Korean movie had a budget of 12 billion (1 Korean Won = 0.00086 Dollars) .

Once those rows were removed, the mean budget was found to be $39,885,003. The vast majority of movies are ones with small budgets with little to no recognition.

Perhaps the most important variable included is the IMDB rating assigned by its users. These ratings are valuable for moviegoers to get an overall consensus of whether or not the movie is worth seeing. However, the ratings tend to be biased toward certain types of movies such as thrillers, older classics, and superhero movies (the top movies include Batman and The Godfather). These ratings average around 6.4. The ratings of the top 250 are calculated according to the Bayesian score

rating = (v ÷ (v+25,000)) × R + (25,000 ÷ (v+25,000)) × 7 where R is the average score and v is the number of votes.

Many other interesting variables are included in the data such as Facebook likes, actor Facebook likes, and number of users who rated the movie are also included. The interesting relationships between quantitative variables, such as whether duration correlates with gross revenue, or whether more likes mean a better score, can be condensed into one large matrix plot below:

The most obviously visible correlation is the exponential relationship between the number of votes and the IMDB score, but given that the number of votes is a variable in the formula for the score, this means very little. The number of votes and the gross revenue have a positive correlation (r = 0.634), which makes sense because one would think that if more people are rating the movie, more people are buying tickets to see it. The year of release and the number of votes show a slight increasing trend, meaning that more and more people are rating movies through IMDB, but the relationship is not significant (p-­‐value= 0.5552, R^2 < 0.0001). Year and gross revenue seem to hint at a growing curve, but this is likely due to inflation. Facebook likes and votes have an interesting relationship with two diverging paths (a Principal Components Analysis would be necessary to explore further). Gross revenue and score have slightly positive relationship-­‐ one would hope that better

movies are earning more money. The only scatter plot that could suggest a negative relationship is year and score. This might hint that movies are getting worse, or that we look at the past with rose-­‐tinted glasses! The predicted decrease of 0.0186 points with an increase of one year of release is small, but highly significant (p-­‐ value<0.0001).

Then there is the burning question of whether bigger budget movies make more money. They don’t tend to make less money, that much is clear; however there is too much variance and too many outliers to say that bigger budget movies have higher gross revenue. Cast Facebook likes didn’t seem to make a difference, but movies with a budget less than $40,000,000 have very few, probably because casting well-­‐ liked actors adds to the budget significantly.

To resolve the issue of high budgets, I created a new column titled “net”, equal to the gross revenue minus the budget. Aside from advertising costs and merchandising revenue, this is a solid estimate of how much money a movie made. The movies with the highest net revenue are Avatar, Jurassic World, Titanic, and Star Wars A New Hope (which is surprising because it was the very first in the series), and that the Disney movie John Carter lost almost $200,000,000. This time, the movie Facebook likes hung around the outer areas.

The last category I made was titled “ratio”, calculated as the gross revenue divided by the cost. I discovered that the Horror film Paranormal Activity made over 7,000 times its budget of $15,000 (other movies up there were mainly horror). Now the trend of Facebook likes is finally visible-­‐ Movies with a budget higher than around 100,000,000 have significantly more likes. Black-­‐and-­‐White movies are small budget (though this may be due to inflation) and have less net revenue, but they still can have impressively high ratios-­‐ the two notable examples are Gone With the Wind and Snow White.

On to the categorical data, Black and White movies have a mean IMDB rating of 7.22, while color movies have a mean rating of 6.38. A two-­‐sample t test proves to be highly significant (t=12.1). Before we hastily conclude that movies used to be better, it would be wise to remember that only the best films of the past are remembered (and make it onto this dataset, which is 96% color movies).

The same analysis was conducted for English vs. Foreign films (25% of the data). A t test was performed and found that foreign films were rated more poorly (p-­‐value = 0.02672). A Wilcoxon Rank-­‐Sum test had similar findings.

An Analysis of Variance was conducted across content ratings (G, PG, PG13, R), and the null hypothesis of all group means being equal was rejected. The following is an ANOVA table.

The above graph shows the 95% confidence intervals for the difference each group mean, using Tukey’s Honest Significant Differences method. It is apparent that R-­‐ rated movies are significantly higher-­‐rated than PG or PG-­‐13 movies.

Many have already used predictive models to guess a movies’ IMDB score and gross revenue to varying degrees of success. As there is a wealth of that material already accessible, I have chosen to do something more unpredictable: create a predictive model that guesses what rating I will give a movie. Ultimately, such a model could be useful in discovering new movies for myself to watch. Netflix and other services use these kinds of models to give recommendations.

I have spent a great deal of time rating every single movie I have seen in order to train the model. I have seen 336 of the 5,031 movies in the dataset; when added to films not in the dataset and films I was too young to remember watching, this figure could easily exceed 400. Based on the previous mean movie duration, I have spent at least 720 hours (30 days!) of my life watching movies. In case you are interested, my highest rated movies are Memento, The Incredibles, American Beauty, Airplane, Birdman, and The Good The Bad and The Ugly.

As you can see in the above plot, my ratings more or less tend to follow the IMDB ratings. For each increase in the IMDB rating, you can expect my rating to increase by 1.21219 on average (The 95% confidence interval is [1.045091 1.379293]).

To build the predictive model, I added new columns for every genre, with a “1” if the movie is of that genre. Movies could be of multiple genres. I also split the data of movies I had rated into train (308 movies) and test (34 movies).

A multiple regression gets complicated with so many variables. After playing around with various combinations, the best and most succinct fit is the model my_rating = duration + imdbscore + thriller. The adjusted R^2 was 0.39. A list of predictors is below.

Due to the many possible variables and interactions, multiple regression is not the best tool to use-­‐ instead I will rely on K-­‐Nearest-­‐Neighbors.

K-­‐Nearest-­‐Neighbors at k = 3 yielded a sum of squared error of 148.28. At k = 4, SSE was 100.37, at k = 5, 97.72, at k= 6, 98.74, and at k= 7, 140.38. The trend is visibly increasing so we can stop.

Thus our final model is k = 5 to minimize the sum of squared error.

The top movies under the model were The Good The Bad and The Ugly, Reservoir Dogs, Star Wars Episode 5, Home Alone, The Dark Knight, and Casino Royale. I love The Good The Bad and The Ugly, Star Wars, and Dark Knight. I like Home Alone and Casino Royale. I dislike Reservoir Dogs, though the recommendation makes sense because people have told me I should like it. Not too bad!

The model failed to recommend Some Like it Hot, which it predicted I would rate a 5. The model also did a good job of rating the bad movies poorly.
